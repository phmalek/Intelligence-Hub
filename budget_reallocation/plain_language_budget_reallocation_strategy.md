# Plain-Language Budget Reallocation Strategy

## Starting Point

The goal is not to move budget because a spreadsheet says a market has a high score.

The goal is to move budget based on a simple planning question:

> Where is the next euro most likely to create the next useful lead?

This should be explainable to any strategist without talking about models, algorithms, or complex scoring.

## The Four Questions

### 1. Where Is Demand Already Responding?

Look at recent weeks by market and ask:

- Are people visiting the site?
- Are they configuring cars?
- Are they submitting dealer contact forms?
- Is cost per lead improving or getting worse?
- Is spend going up while leads stay flat?

Markets where spend is producing good leads should be protected.

Markets where spend is increasing but response is not improving should be challenged.

Simple rule:

> If a market is converting efficiently and still has room to grow, give it more money.

### 2. Where Are We Wasting Pressure?

Some markets may be receiving budget, but the extra pressure is not creating extra response.

Examples:

- Spend increased, but leads did not increase.
- Cost per lead is getting worse week after week.
- The market has low volume and low conversion.
- We are paying for traffic but not getting dealer actions.

Simple rule:

> If extra spend is not creating extra response, stop feeding it automatically.

This does not mean turning the market off. It means reducing flexible budget and keeping only the necessary base level.

### 3. Where Is There Headroom?

The cheapest market is not always the best market to scale.

Some markets may not have the lowest cost per lead today, but they may still have a bigger opportunity.

Signals of headroom:

- Strong vehicle sales potential.
- Good website engagement.
- Low current media pressure.
- Past weeks show that when we spend, the market responds.
- The market can realistically absorb more budget.

Simple rule:

> Do not only reward the cheapest market. Reward markets that can still grow.

This avoids putting all flexible budget into small efficient markets that cannot scale further.

### 4. What Changed This Week?

Budget should react weekly, not just once at the start of the campaign.

Each week, every market should fall into one of five planning buckets.

| Bucket | Meaning | Budget Action |
|---|---|---|
| Scale | Good performance and room to grow | Increase budget |
| Protect | Good performance but limited room | Keep budget steady |
| Fix | Opportunity exists, but performance is weak | Keep modest budget, change channel/message |
| Reduce | Weak performance and weak response | Move budget out |
| Watch | Not enough evidence | Keep small test budget |

## The Strategy In One Sentence

> We increase markets that are responding and still have room, protect markets that are working, reduce markets where extra spend is not creating leads, and keep small test budgets where the evidence is unclear.

## How To Talk About Reallocation

Avoid saying:

> Market A has a score of 78.

Say:

> Market A is getting more budget because it is producing leads efficiently, the trend is improving, and it still has enough volume to absorb more spend.

Avoid saying:

> Market B is being penalised by the model.

Say:

> Market B is being reduced because extra spend has not translated into extra dealer contact forms over the last few weeks.

Avoid saying:

> The algorithm recommends shifting budget.

Say:

> We are moving flexible budget from markets where media pressure is not converting into markets where recent demand signals are stronger.

## Example Weekly Reallocation Story

This week, we are shifting flexible budget out of markets where cost per lead has worsened for several weeks and lead volume has not grown. We are moving it into markets where recent spend has produced dealer contact forms efficiently and where traffic suggests there is still demand to capture.

We are not cutting weaker markets completely. We are keeping a base level where needed and asking whether the issue is budget, channel mix, message, landing page quality, or local market readiness.

## Practical Rules For The App

The app should not only show a ranking. It should explain the reason behind each recommendation.

Useful recommendation labels:

- `Scale`: strong response, room to grow.
- `Protect`: working well, maintain pressure.
- `Reduce`: spend is not producing enough response.
- `Fix`: opportunity exists, but current setup is not working.
- `Watch`: not enough evidence yet.

Each recommendation should answer three questions:

- What changed recently?
- Why does that matter?
- What should we do with budget this week?

## What The Strategist Should See

For each market, the strategist should be able to read a plain sentence like:

> Increase budget in Market A because dealer contact forms are growing, cost per lead is improving, and the market still has enough volume to scale.

Or:

> Reduce flexible budget in Market B because spend increased over recent weeks but dealer contact forms did not follow.

Or:

> Keep Market C on watch because the evidence is mixed: traffic is improving, but lead conversion has not caught up yet.

## Guiding Principle

The budget should follow evidence, but the explanation should sound like media planning.

Behind the scenes, the app can calculate performance, trend, efficiency, and headroom. In front of the team and the client, the story should stay simple:

> More budget where response is strong and scalable. Less budget where pressure is not converting. Small tests where evidence is still unclear.
